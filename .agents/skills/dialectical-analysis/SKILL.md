# Dialectical Analysis

Applied when a subject has a thesis that can be opposed — evaluating proposals, validating claims, assessing arguments. Not a universal thinking stance; do not apply to determinate technical questions with clear answers.

## Ground the Proposition Before Taking Sides

Before generating the antithesis, resolve the proposition into its load-bearing claims and the grounds those claims rest on. Verify that the core claim holds from the problem's intrinsic constraints rather than from convention or analogy.

Gotcha: Generating a counter-argument against a poorly-understood thesis produces a straw man by default. Reconstruct the strongest version of the original claim first — then oppose it.

## Bilateral Stress-Testing — Two Independent Tracks

Actively generate and evaluate the antithesis. Run verification on two separate tracks:

- **Information track**: Is the empirical data accurate? Are sources valid, current, and applicable to this context?
- **Logic track**: Is the structure sound? Are there fallacies, hidden assumptions, or invalid inferences?

Gotcha: Conflating data errors with logic errors produces unfalsifiable critiques. A claim can be logically valid but empirically false, or empirically supported but logically unsound. Name which failure mode applies.

## Steel-Manning (Anti-Contrarianism)

Stress-test against the strongest possible version of the opposing view — not a weakened caricature. If the original argument's logic and data are robust after steel-manning, acknowledge this explicitly rather than forcing an artificial debate.

Gotcha: Contrarianism for its own sake is not rigor. A straw-man counter-argument signals intellectual dishonesty and undermines the evaluation's credibility. The goal is truth-tracking, not adversarial performance.

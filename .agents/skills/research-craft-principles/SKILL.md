# Research Craft Principles

Applied when producing analytical or research work. These are the domain translation of implementation quality principles.

## Clarity and Directness (≈ Readability)

State the claim before the evidence. Lead with conclusions; support afterward. Avoid burying the thesis in qualifications.

Gotcha: Academic hedging ≠ rigor. Precision is achieved through explicit assumptions, not vague qualifiers like "may" or "could potentially".

## Document Methodology Choices, Not Just Steps (≈ Self-Explanatory Code)

Write *why* a method was chosen, not just *what* was done. "We used linear regression" is a step; "We used linear regression because the relationship is expected to be monotone and the sample size makes nonparametric methods underpowered" is methodology documentation.

Gotcha: The reader already knows what the tool does — explain why it was the right tool here.

## Explicit Assumptions and Testable Claims (≈ Verifiable Robustness)

State all assumptions explicitly. Every claim should be either directly evidenced or explicitly flagged as inference. Prefer falsifiable over unfalsifiable assertions.

Gotcha: Do not bury load-bearing assumptions inside methodology sections or leave them implicit in data choices. Surface them as named constraints: "This analysis assumes X; if X fails, conclusion Y does not hold."

## One Claim Per Unit (≈ Short Focused Functions)

Each paragraph or section makes one contribution. If a section title requires "and", split it.

Gotcha: Multi-claim paragraphs make the argument hard to trace, cite, or challenge. Splitting also forces clarity about whether the claims are actually distinct or redundant.

## State Limitations Upfront (≈ Guard Clauses)

Declare scope boundaries and known limitations before the main analysis. Do not hide them in a Discussion section epilogue.

Gotcha: Limitations buried at the end read as defensive. Limitations stated upfront read as rigorous. Readers (and reviewers) respect pre-declared constraints.

## Unified Argument — No Special-Case Carve-Outs (≈ Unified Logic Paths)

If a counter-example forces a special-case sub-argument, revise the general theory to accommodate it rather than patching with "except in the case of X".

Gotcha: A theory that requires n carve-outs is n hypotheses, not one. The carve-out is signal that the abstraction (model, framework, taxonomy) is at the wrong level of generality.

## Minimize Scope Creep (≈ Minimize Variable Scope)

A focused research question is answered. A broad one is gestured at. Narrow the question until it can be fully addressed by the available evidence and methods.

Gotcha: "We leave this to future work" is only acceptable if the omitted scope was never claimed. If it was in the original question, it is a scope failure, not a limitation.
